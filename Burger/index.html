Chees Burger

Many food historians credit 16-year-old Lionel Sternberger, who in 1924 decided to slap a slice of American cheese (what else?) onto a cooking hamburger at his father's Pasadena, California, sandwich shop, the Rite Spot. He liked it, and so did his dad, and thus the cheeseburger was born. Or was it? They called it a "cheese hamburger," so that may disqualify the Sternbergers on a technicality. The first sandwich to actually be called a "cheeseburger" was at Kaelin's restaurant in Louisville, Kentucky. Charles Kaelin claims to have invented the cheese-topped burger in 1934 because he wanted, reports Louisville writer Robin Garr, to "add a new tang to the hamburger." This is the earliest example of a menu claiming to be "the birthplace of the cheeseburger."

Veggie Burger

Veggie burgers come in so many varieties, from tofu-based to zucchini-based, that it can be hard to nail down a definition of what “counts” as a veggie burger. For all practical purposes, a veggie burger is one that contains no meat. Rather, veggie burgers can be made with any number of plant-based foods, such as grains, beans, soy, or (of course) vegetables. Because of their diversity of ingredients, plant-based burgers range in nutrition. The patty you whip up at home out of tofu, black beans, and/or quinoa will likely be full of fiber, plant-based protein, and micronutrients. A veggie burger.
